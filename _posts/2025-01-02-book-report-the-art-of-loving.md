## Introduction: is love an art?

In this post, I will share my thoughts and reflections on the book "The Art of Loving" by Erich Fromm. I read this book for preparing my English book club meeting to be held next week. Before diving into the main ideas of the book, I first want to say that the *love* in this book is not really the love that we usually think of: romantic love. Instead, the author uses the word *love* as a general term for the relationship between humans and the world, as a mean to answer the profound problem of human existence.

The book starts with a question: "Is love an art?". By deeming love as an art, it means that (the author argues that) love is a skill that can be learned and practiced. If love is an art, by all means, we should learn it and practice it. On the other hand, if love is not an art, then we don't need to try hard to learn it and practice it, but we feel it and follow the our gut to achieve it.

This book starts with the argument that love is an art, which we intentionally try to learn and practice. And the author says that he is going to unfold the love in aspects of theory and practice. Every art has its own theory and practice. To be an expert of an art, we first need to understand the theory so that we understands it. But just understanding the theory is not enough, we also need to practice it. Even if we understand the theory, even if we know how to do it, it is never enough to implement it. We need to practice it with the theory. That is, the author argues that love is an art, and we should learn it and practice it. One thing the author emphasizes is that, to learn and practice love, we need to actually put our time and concern into it with all sincerity.

And the following chapters are those theories and practices of love, the author tries to unfold.

## Summary

Before diving into my thoughts on the book, I want to summarize the book with few concepts that the author suggests. By understanding these concepts, we can have a better understanding of the book, thus I can share and argue my thoughts on the book more effectively.

### What is love?

One of the main concept that the author argues is how the love originates. The author argues that love originates as a solution to the problem of human existence: how we can be free from the problem of being separated from the world. He argues that, we as a human being, we are born with a natural tendency to be connected to the world. This natural tendency is manifested as several forms in the history of human civilization. Some of them are pursuing orgy, seeking targets for conformity, or exercising creativity with our very imagination. However, these forms of love are not enough to solve the problem of being separated from the world. And the author now argues that the true solution to this problem is love.

Also the author stresses that love is not just receiving, but also giving. These components of love are easily separated from each other, but in the author's view, they are inseparable and should be come together in order to be a true love.

**Elements of love:**
The author suggests four fundamental elements that must exist together for true love: *care*, *responsibility*, *respect*, and *knowledge*. *Care* manifests as a deep concern for the well-being of the loved one. *Responsibility* involves taking ownership of their impact on relationships, responding to both expressed and unexpressed needs. *Respect* means recognizing the unique individuality of the partner, allowing them to respect each other. *Knowledge* represents the continuous effort to deeply understand the loved one.

**Type of love:**
The author also suggests that there are several types of love: *brotherly love*, *parental love*, *erotic love*, and *the love of god*, including *the love of self*. *Brotherly love* is the love towards multiple people, where it is within the equal relationship. *Parental love* is the love based on the idea of providing comfort, safety, and protection for a helpless being. *Erotic love* is the love towards a single person, which is based on unity and merging with that person. *Love of god* is based on the need for unity and belonging. Fromm viewed it as a path from unconditional love to conscious choice, and as a search for oneself and individual self-development. Also there is *the love of self*, which is the love towards oneself.


### The Practice of Love

One thing this book is saying is that this book does not provide a prescription for love. Rather, it provides a guide to love. The author suggests that the practice of love is discipline, concentration, patience, and supreme concern. This basically means that we need to put our time and effort into love, and we need to be patient and have a supreme concern for the other.

## Reflections

This book talks about love in a way that is not easy to understand. And I think I could think a lot of aspects of love from this book. Now, I want to share my thoughts on the book with a few points.

### 1. Love can be categorized, but it is not the thing fixed.

First, I think the author's separation of motherly and fatherly love is an oversimplification of parental love's complex nature. From what I believe, parents can simultaneously express both unconditional nurturing (traditionally labeled as motherly) and conditional growth-oriented love (traditionally labeled as fatherly). And it is manifested based on the needs, a variety of situations, etc. Also, these aspects of love aren't fixed or gender-specific traits, but rather capabilities that any parent can develop and express. Based on the needs, I think it is possible to express both motherly and fatherly love at the same time. Furthermore, just as children grow and change through their interactions with the world, the children can also grow and change their love towards the ideal being. That is, the idea that we simply need both types of parental love misses how one can grow and improve upon their external environment.


### 2. How can I define true love, and how can I perceive it?

I think every person learn things through their own lens of experience. In this sense, I also think the author's theory of true love is based on his personal experience, much like how we all come to understand love through our own lives. His theory emphasizes that authentic love involves both receiving and giving, extending beyond just romantic connections. While his perspective is theoretical, it offers valuable insights into how love manifests in various forms - whether between parents and children, friends, or partners. And I believe this broader view helps us understand love as a universal force that encompasses multiple types of relationships and expressions.

### 3. Every aspect of life can be seen as art.

The author's view of love as an art form resonates me with a deeper relationship with artistry. That is, I think artistry exists in all aspects of life. Just as Dao philosophy sees life itself as an act of learning which appears in all aspects of life, I think love too can be a practice we refine through experience and conscious engagement. I think art is the practice that requires dedication, presence, and a willingness to learn through *experience* with a theory we have and acquire through our life *experience*. And the love is the same.

## Conclusion

This is the conclusion of my thoughts on the book "The Art of Loving". While this book came out in 1956, I think the author's view of love is still relevant to the modern society. I think the author's view of love is a good guide for us to understand love and practice it, and also it gave me a lot of insights on love.
