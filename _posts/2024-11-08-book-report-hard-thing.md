## Introduction

When I was almost finishing one season for Trevari, I realized that I need to use Trevari's 'Going Out' program before it expires. Yes, I could have just ignored it, but I thought it would be a good opportunity to explore new topics, where I could learn something new. Because I've been told that every group has its own unique culture, and I also wanted to try something different from my group. After searching for a while, I found 'Entrepreneurs: How to Start a Startup' to be the most interesting one for me, because I've always been interested in startups and entrepreneurship. So that was where It all started.

"The Hard Thing About Hard Things" is a business book written by Ben Horowitz, a successful Silicon Valley entrepreneur, based on his experiences of failures and successes. Unlike typical business books, this book doesn't present simple formulas for success like other business books. Instead, it sincerely shares moments of failure and frustration, along with the lessons and wisdom gained from those experiences. And I think this is what makes this book so valuable.

Without further ado, already assuming that you've read the book, I want to add my own thoughts and reflections on the book. And these are some of my insights from the book.

## (Some of my) Insights

### Even with good people and good company, you could still fail
I have always thought that luck plays a significant role in the success of a company. This book reinforces that belief by showing that even with good people and a good company, failure is still possible. For instance, Ben Horowitz, the author, shares his experience running Loudcloud, a cloud services company. Despite having a talented team and a solid business plan, they were severely impacted by the dot-com bubble burst. To make matters worse, Microsoft decided to enter the cloud services market, creating even more challenges for Loudcloud. Specifically, they had to lay off a large portion of people and pivot their business model to survive. And the key takeaway here is that even with good people and a good company, you could still fail. And I thought this is where the one of the roles of a CEO comes in, that is, first to be prepared to meet the very hard challenges, and second, to be prepared to make tough decisions and pivot quickly when necessary to navigate through difficult times.

### Transparency, trust, and culture
These are the ones that I have been thinking about for a long time. And this book has reinforced my belief on these as well. For example, I have been thinking that I need to be as transparent as possible when dealing with my colleagues. And this is because of effective communication. I think that we, as a human, always try to understand the other person judging by our own experiences. That being said, we could always misunderstand the meaning of the other person. If we are not transparent, we could even more misunderstand the other person. And this is why I think transparency is so important.

With transparency, trust is built. Once you have transparency, people will trust you because they know what you are doing and why you are doing it (I often express it as "sync with the person" because this means that I can share my thoughts and feelings with the other person without telling them). In this sense, trust is not just the beliving in the other person for no reason, but the beliving in the other person because they know what you are doing and why you are doing it.

And last but not least, once you have established trust, you can now build a strong culture within the company. Trust serves as the foundation upon which a strong organizational culture is built, fostering collaboration, innovation, and a shared sense of purpose among team members.

One thing I want to add is that this is the reason why the leader's behavior is so important. If the leader is transparent, the colleagues will be transparent. If the leader is not transparent, the colleagues will not be transparent. Culture is something that everyone in the company can experience, and it is shaped by the leader's actions.

### Management debt
The author also mentioned management debt, which is the accumulated problems that hinder the company's progress. Examples of management debt include hiring the wrong people, not addressing performance issues, and failing to establish clear processes and policies. These issues can accumulate over time, creating significant obstacles that can impede the company's growth and success. From what I have been experiencing, I think this is a very important concept to understand. Because these are things that we often ignore, but they could be the ones that lead to the company's failure. What the author mentioned is that the leader should be aware of management debt. And I think this is very important. Because if the leader is not aware of it, he could make the situation even worse.

As an engineer, I have been thinking about technical debt a lot as well. Technical debt (which I think is a more famous and notorious term) is the cost of additional rework caused by choosing an easy solution now instead of using a better approach that would take longer. It accumulates over time and can significantly slow down development, making it harder to implement changes and fix bugs. Similarly, management debt refers to the accumulation of poor management decisions that can hinder a company's progress. Both types of debt can be detrimental if not addressed well.

In the end, I think one of the most important things for a leader is to be aware of management debt. To be aware of it, the leader needs to intentionally think about every behavior and decision he makes, and finds for ways to reduce them.

### Scale-up
Scale-up is also an interesting topic. Because as a company grows, the ability required for the colleagues also changes. For instance, when a company is small, the colleagues are required to be very versatile and hands-on. But as the company grows, they need to develop expertise in specific areas. This transition requires continuous training and development to ensure that the team can meet the new demands and challenges that come with growth. Therefore, investing in employee training and development is crucial for a successful scale-up.

I think it is also important to be aware of the changes in the ability required for the colleagues for the leaders as well. That being said, hiring or promoting (or training) the right people to meet the new demands and challenges that come with growth is crucial for a successful scale-up.
